UX Design Fundamentals

Design thinking, research, wireframing, visual hierarchy, and building a design system

UX (User Experience) design is the process of creating products that provide meaningful and relevant experiences to users. It is not about how something looks — it is about how something works. A beautiful website that confuses users is a failure. An ugly website that gets out of the way and lets users accomplish their goals is a success. The best engineers understand UX deeply because they make hundreds of decisions every day that affect their users.

UX vs UI: What Is the Difference?

The terms are often used interchangeably but they describe different things:

UX (User Experience)UI (User Interface)
FocusHow the product works and feelsHow the product looks and is arranged
QuestionsCan users find what they need? Is the flow logical?Are the colors consistent? Is the spacing balanced?
OutputsWireframes, user flows, research reports, prototypesMockups, style guides, component specs
ToolsFigma (wireframes), Miro, paper sketchesFigma (high-fidelity), Illustrator, Photoshop

In small teams, one person often does both. The distinction matters because they require different thinking modes: UX is logic and empathy; UI is taste and craft.

Design Thinking Process

Design thinking is a structured approach to solving problems. It is not linear — you loop back to earlier stages as you learn. The five stages are:

Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test     ↓           ↓          ↓         ↓        ↓ Understand users → Define problem → Brainstorm → Build quick → Get feedback                                         ← Iterate ←
  1. Empathize — Conduct user interviews, observe behavior, understand pain points. You are not the user. Your assumptions are wrong until validated.
  2. Define — Synthesize research into a clear problem statement: "Travelers need a way to compare flight prices without opening ten tabs because they feel overwhelmed by choice."
  3. Ideate — Brainstorm solutions. Quantity over quality at this stage. Sketch multiple approaches. The first idea is rarely the best.
  4. Prototype — Build a low-fidelity version. Paper sketch, clickable wireframe, or basic HTML. Just enough to test the concept.
  5. Test — Put the prototype in front of real users. Watch where they hesitate. Listen to their questions. Then go back to step 2 or 3.
You Are Already Doing Design Thinking

If you have ever built a feature, showed it to someone, watched them struggle, and then changed it — you did design thinking. The formal process just makes it intentional and repeatable. The biggest mistake is skipping the Empathize and Test stages because they require talking to humans.

User Research Methods

Research prevents building the wrong thing. Use these methods depending on what you need to learn:

MethodWhen to UseEffort
User interviewsUnderstand motivations, pain points, mental modelsHigh
SurveysGather quantitative data at scaleMedium
Usability testingWatch real users try to complete tasks on your productHigh
Analytics reviewSee where users drop off, what they click, where they get stuckLow (data exists)
Competitive analysisLearn what works (and does not) in similar productsLow
Card sortingDetermine how users expect information to be organizedMedium

Rule of thumb: Watch three users use your product. The first three will reveal 80% of the biggest issues. Do not wait until you have 50 testers — three is enough to start.

Information Architecture

Information architecture (IA) is how you organize and label content. Good IA means users find what they need without thinking. Bad IA means users scan the page three times before clicking something wrong.

Key IA Principles

  • Hierarchy — Group related items. Primary navigation, secondary navigation, content. No more than 5-7 top-level navigation items (Miller's Law).
  • Label clearly — "Products" is better than "Solutions Portfolio". Users scan labels, they do not decode them.
  • Progressive disclosure — Show the minimum needed for the current task. Reveal advanced options only when requested.
  • Consistency — Same action, same label, same location across the entire product.

Navigation Patterns

PatternWhen to Use
Top navigation bar5-7 top-level pages. Most common for marketing sites.
Sidebar navigationMany sections (dashboards, docs, admin panels).
Hamburger menuMobile — frees screen space but hides navigation. Use as a last resort or for secondary nav.
BreadcrumbsDeep hierarchies (e-commerce categories, documentation). Shows where the user is.
TabsSwitching between views of the same content type (settings sections, profile views).
Card Sorting Exercise

Write every content type and feature on sticky notes. Ask 3-5 people to group them into categories. The groups that emerge naturally are your navigation structure. Do not invent a structure — discover it from how users think.

Wireframing and Prototyping

A wireframe is a low-fidelity layout that shows structure, not polish. No colors, no images, no typography decisions — just boxes, lines, and labels. A prototype is a clickable version of a wireframe that simulates the user flow.

Fidelity Levels

FidelityFormPurpose
LowPaper sketch or bare Figma framesExplore layout options quickly. Cheap to change.
MediumGrayscale wireframes with real content placementTest navigation and information hierarchy.
HighPixel-perfect mockups with colors, typography, iconsFinal sign-off, developer handoff, visual testing.

The rule: Start at the lowest fidelity that answers your current question. Do not polish a layout until you have validated the structure. Changing a wireframe takes minutes. Changing a high-fidelity mockup takes hours. Changing production code takes days.

Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy is the arrangement of elements in order of importance. Users do not read — they scan. Hierarchy guides their eyes to what matters first.

Six Tools for Creating Hierarchy

ToolHow It Works
SizeLarger elements are more important. Headings larger than body text.
ColorBright or high-contrast elements draw attention. Muted colors recede.
SpacingMore space around an element signals importance. Whitespace is not wasted space.
PositionTop-left (in Western reading patterns) is the primary attention zone.
WeightBold text stands out from regular text. Thin weights fade into the background.
MotionAnimating elements draw immediate attention. Use sparingly.
Hierarchy through CSS — before and after
/* Before — everything looks the same */
.card { font-size: 16px; padding: 12px; border: 1px solid #ddd; }

/* After — clear hierarchy */
.card-title   { font-size: 1.25rem; font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.25rem; }
.card-meta    { font-size: 0.875rem; color: #666; margin-bottom: 0.75rem; }
.card-body    { font-size: 1rem; line-height: 1.5; }
.card-cta     { display: inline-block; background: blue; color: white; padding: 0.5rem 1rem; }

Color Theory for the Web

Color communicates meaning, creates hierarchy, and establishes brand identity. You do not need to be a designer to use color effectively — you need a system.

The 60-30-10 Rule

A simple formula for balanced color usage:

  • 60% dominant/neutral color — background, large areas
  • 30% secondary color — headers, cards, UI elements
  • 10% accent color — buttons, links, calls to action

Color Roles

RolePurposeExample
PrimaryMain brand color, used for key interactive elementsButton background, link color
SecondarySupporting color, complements primarySecondary buttons, badges
NeutralText, backgrounds, bordersGrayscale palette (50-900)
SuccessPositive feedbackGreen for confirmation messages
WarningCaution feedbackAmber for validation warnings
ErrorNegative feedbackRed for validation errors
InfoInformational feedbackBlue for informational banners
Never Rely on Color Alone

Approximately 8% of men have some form of color blindness (red-green is most common). Never convey information using color alone — always include text labels, icons, or patterns. A required field should have a label AND an asterisk, not just a red border. Error states should include text AND color.

Typography

Typography is the single biggest factor in perceived quality. Bad typography makes a site feel amateur. Good typography makes it feel trustworthy — even if the content is the same.

Key Typography Decisions

DecisionGuideline
Font stackUse system fonts (-apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", Roboto) for performance. Use web fonts sparingly — each font file adds load time.
Font sizesUse a type scale: 0.75rem, 0.875rem, 1rem, 1.25rem, 1.5rem, 2rem, 2.5rem, 3rem. Avoid arbitrary sizes.
Line heightBody text: 1.5-1.7. Headings: 1.1-1.3. Too tight = unreadable. Too loose = disconnected.
Line length45-75 characters per line. Set max-width: 65ch on text containers.
Font pairingOne font for headings, one for body. Or use weights/italic of the same font family. Never use more than two fonts.
A readable typography system in CSS
body {
  font-family: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", Roboto, sans-serif;
  font-size: 1rem;
  line-height: 1.6;
  color: #1a1a2e;
}

h1 { font-size: 2.5rem; line-height: 1.2; font-weight: 700; }
h2 { font-size: 2rem;   line-height: 1.25; font-weight: 600; }
h3 { font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.3; font-weight: 600; }
h4 { font-size: 1.25rem; line-height: 1.4; font-weight: 500; }

p, li { max-width: 65ch; }  /* readable line length */

Design Systems

A design system is a single source of truth for visual and functional patterns. It includes colors, typography, spacing, components, and guidelines. Every mature product has one — formal or informal.

Design Tokens

Design tokens are the atomic values that make up a design system. They bridge the gap between design tools and code:

Design tokens as CSS custom properties
:root {
  /* Color tokens */
  --color-primary: #2563eb;
  --color-primary-hover: #1d4ed8;
  --color-text: #1a1a2e;
  --color-text-secondary: #64748b;
  --color-bg: #ffffff;
  --color-bg-subtle: #f8fafc;

  /* Spacing tokens */
  --space-xs: 0.25rem;   /* 4px */
  --space-sm: 0.5rem;    /* 8px */
  --space-md: 1rem;      /* 16px */
  --space-lg: 1.5rem;    /* 24px */
  --space-xl: 2rem;      /* 32px */
  --space-2xl: 3rem;     /* 48px */

  /* Typography tokens */
  --font-body: system-ui, sans-serif;
  --font-heading: system-ui, sans-serif;
  --text-sm: 0.875rem;
  --text-base: 1rem;
  --text-lg: 1.125rem;
  --text-xl: 1.25rem;

  /* Border radius tokens */
  --radius-sm: 4px;
  --radius-md: 8px;
  --radius-lg: 12px;

  /* Shadow tokens */
  --shadow-sm: 0 1px 2px rgba(0,0,0,0.05);
  --shadow-md: 0 4px 6px rgba(0,0,0,0.1);
}

Component Library

Above tokens sit components: Button, Input, Card, Modal, etc. Each component documents its states (default, hover, active, disabled, loading, error) and variations (primary, secondary, ghost, danger). A design system without a component library is a style guide. A component library without a design system is inconsistent.

Start Small

Do not build a full design system on day one. Start with a color palette and spacing scale in CSS custom properties. Add components as you repeat patterns. A Button component after the third time you write <button class="btn btn-primary"> is justified. A Button component before you have a single page is premature abstraction.

Common UX Anti-Patterns

Anti-PatternWhy It HurtsFix
Dark patterns (tricking users)Destroy trust. Users will leave and never return.Be transparent. Make unsubscribing as easy as subscribing.
Too many choicesHick's Law: more choices = longer decision time.Guide users. Recommend the best option. Progressive disclosure.
No feedback after actionsUsers wonder if the system registered their click.Show loading states, success messages, error messages. Every action needs a reaction.
Inconsistent patternsUsers must relearn how to use each page.Define patterns once. Use them everywhere. Same button placement, same label style.
Hidden navigationUsers cannot find what they need.Important actions should be visible. Do not bury primary navigation behind clicks.
Assuming mobile = less contentMobile users need the same content, not less of it.Responsive layout, not responsive content removal.
Junior Developer

How a Junior Might Think

  • Thinks UX is "making it look pretty"
  • Ships features without testing with anyone
  • Uses random colors and font sizes ("it looks fine to me")
  • Does not understand why users struggle with their UI
  • Skipped research — builds what they think users want
  • Treats a design system as "overhead" instead of a productivity tool
Senior Developer

How a Senior Thinks

  • Knows UX is about outcomes, not aesthetics
  • Tests early and often — watches real users interact
  • Uses a systematic approach to color, typography, and spacing
  • Designs for the user who is distracted, tired, and on a phone
  • Builds a design system incrementally as patterns emerge
  • Asks "what problem are we solving?" before writing any code
